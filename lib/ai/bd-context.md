# AOSN Business Development Outreach Context
## Veterinary Specialty Practice Acquisition

---

## Who We Are

**AOSN (Animal Outpatient Specialty Network)** is a PE-backed veterinary specialty practice consolidation platform (backed by Percheron Capital) operating 55+ locations across 19 states. AOSN was rebranded from Animal Dermatology Group (ADG) in March 2025 — ADG was founded as a dermatology-only platform; AOSN represents the expansion into a broader outpatient specialty model. We acquire and partner with independent veterinary specialty practices across disciplines including dermatology, dentistry, ophthalmology, surgery, oncology, internal medicine, cardiology, and neurology.

**Our value proposition to practice owners:**
- Liquidity event / partial or full monetization of years of practice building
- Operational support so doctors can focus on medicine, not business
- Network of peers across specialties
- Continuity of care and staff culture preserved post-acquisition
- Ongoing employment and clinical autonomy for the selling doctor

---

## Market Context & Competitive Landscape

- The veterinary specialty market is approximately **75% consolidated** (combined Emergency & Specialty segment; source: Brakke Consulting)
- The majority of private specialty practices have been absorbed by large corporate consolidators: **Blue Pearl, VCA, Mars-owned entities, NVA, Mission Pet Health (formerly SVP), and Thrive**
- **Thrive** operates two dedicated branded segments: **D4A (Dermatology for Animals)** and **O4A (Ophthalmology for Animals)** — directly competing for AOSN's target specialties
- **Mission Pet Health** is an active competitor currently pursuing the same independent outpatient specialty practices AOSN is targeting
- Most corporate consolidators do not rebrand acquired practices, making it hard to track true ownership — Mars is a notable exception
- **"Corporate" is a four-letter word in this market.** The consolidation wave has left deep anti-corporate sentiment among practice owners, associates, and staff. Doctors have watched colleagues absorbed by large consolidators and seen clinical autonomy, culture, and staff wellbeing sacrificed. AOSN must consistently position itself as fundamentally different — demonstrated through authentic voices of doctors who have already joined, not corporate messaging alone.

### Key TAM Facts

## Critical Market & Behavioral Facts

Three facts shape every outreach and conversion decision:

1. **Doctors are clinicians first.** Specialist veterinarians are busy seeing patients most of their day. Based on AOSN's experience, doctors check email approximately once a week or less. Channels that reach doctors outside their inbox are necessary to supplement email.

2. **Anti-corporate sentiment is pervasive.** For a significant portion of AOSN's target universe, the word "corporate" triggers a negative response before a conversation begins. Peer-to-peer outreach bypasses the corporate filter entirely — a fellow specialist vouching for their experience carries credibility that no corporate communication can replicate.

3. **Veterinarians are deeply protective of their staff.** The team at a specialty practice is considered family. Owners do not want staff knowing they are considering a sale. Outreach channels that route through the practice risk exposing the conversation prematurely and can permanently damage the relationship.

## EBITDA & Financial Model

- Each doctor generates approximately **$1.2M in annual revenue** with an EBITDA margin of **20–23%**, yielding roughly **$240,000–$276,000 EBITDA per doctor**
- Practices with more doctors produce proportionally more EBITDA
- A 4-doctor practice contributes ~$960K–$1.1M EBITDA per close; a single-doctor practice contributes ~$240K–$276K
- **The $18.9M goal cannot be reached primarily through single-doctor closes — the math requires prioritizing Tier 1 multi-doctor practices**
- AOSN typically passes on practices at **10% EBITDA margin or below** with no clear path to growth; the target is **30% proforma margin**

### Practice Viability Considerations
- **Single-doctor practices:** Key Man Risk (sole doctor leaves = entire revenue at risk), valuation alignment difficulty (solo owners often earn more over time than standard valuation models offer), limited growth without hiring, physical space constraints, geographic overlap assessment
- **Newly opened practices:** Not automatically non-viable — key factors are owner age, current EBITDA, and future goals. Older owner with strong early EBITDA and platform interest may be viable near-term
- **Valuation misalignment:** Recurring challenge on both small and large practices. Younger owners with significant runway sometimes seek upfront pricing reflecting future value they'd bring to AOSN, not current practice value — this expectation is not financially feasible

## Outreach Channels & Strategy

### Channel Constraints
- Cold phone calls to practices are NOT used — messages left with staff risk exposing the doctor's interest in selling, which has caused real damage
- Cold in-person visits are not viable — specialists operate by referral appointment only
- Direct mail requires careful execution — standard mail can be screened by gatekeepers
- iPads and personally addressed greeting cards are used specifically because they are treated as personal correspondence by staff

### Active Outreach Channels (Ranked by Effectiveness)
1. **Peer-to-peer outreach** — highest documented conversion rate to first meeting; slowest and least scalable; clinician goodwill must be protected and not over-asked. Currently have dermatologist and dentist advocates but **NO surgeons or ophthalmologists yet** — this is the most important conversion gap
2. **iPad campaign** — personalized iPads preloaded with auto-play video pitch and dermatologist testimonials; foundational campaign launched March 2025
3. **Personalized direct emails** — individual, non-templated emails written specifically for the recipient doctor
4. **Physical mail** — greeting cards, gift cards, iPad covers to Tier 1; 2-day FedEx packages to doctors not opening emails or without email addresses
5. **HubSpot email campaigns** — follow-up sequences including "Did you get your iPad?" cadence
6. **LinkedIn** — connection requests and visibility touchpoints; effective for younger owners
7. **Conference presence** — in-person relationship building and meeting scheduling
8. **CEO direct email** — selective escalation for high-priority non-responsive targets; effective in limited cases, not scalable

### Peer-to-Peer Program
- Referral fee program in place to incentivize clinician participation, ramped up in 2026
- Two operational challenges: dermatologists sometimes submit recruiting leads rather than acquisition leads; and not all acquired practices rebrand so dermatologists may be unaware a practice is no longer independent
- **Priority:** Extend peer-to-peer into Surgery and Ophthalmology by recruiting willing advocates from newly closed practices in those specialties

### Initial Campaign Results
- 36% response rate across iPad and email campaigns (includes all responses: interested, not interested, removal requests)

## What Has Worked

- **Personalized emails** referencing the specific practice, doctor's background, or local market — not template blasts
- **Short, low-ask first messages** — "Would you be open to a 15-minute call?" outperforms long pitch emails
- **Timing around life events** — retirement-age doctors, practices that recently lost a partner, doctors 15+ years into practice
- **Referrals from practices we've already acquired** — a peer vouching for AOSN removes friction immediately
- **Follow-up sequences with spacing** — one email rarely works; a 3–5 touch sequence over 60–90 days performs better
- **Value-first content** — sharing a relevant industry insight or market comp data point increases reply rates
- **iPad campaign** — physical, personalized, bypasses email inbox entirely
- **FedEx 2-day packages** — adds urgency and personal dimension for non-email-responsive targets
- **CEO direct email** — effective in limited cases as escalation tool for high-priority non-responsive targets

---

## What Hasn't Worked

- Generic "we're a growing platform and would love to connect" emails with no personalization
- Contacting front desk staff and asking to be connected to the owner without context
- High-pressure or deal-timeline-driven outreach ("we're looking to close in Q4") — creates distrust
- Over-contacting a target in a short window — doctors talk to each other; reputation matters
- Emailing associates or non-owner partners without first understanding ownership structure
- Cold calling practices — messages with staff risk exposing the doctor's interest; has caused real damage
- Re-engaging Closed Nurture contacts too soon with generic or campaign-style outreach — risks permanently closing doors
- Applying the 74/week cadence uniformly — Closed Nurture contacts need longer intervals, not a 3-week campaign cadence

## Re-Engagement Tactics for Non-Responders

### Tier 1 — Soft Re-Touch (1–3 prior contacts, no response)
- Send a new email with a different angle (e.g., market update, recent AOSN news, referral mention)
- Try a different medium: LinkedIn connection request with a brief note
- Wait 30–45 days between touches
- Do NOT reference prior outreach in a pressuring way ("I've emailed you several times…")

### Tier 2 — Creative Re-Engagement (3–5 prior contacts, no response)
- Leverage a warm referral if any connection exists in the network
- Invite to a non-sales touchpoint: webinar, industry event, informal peer dinner
- Reach out around a relevant trigger: practice anniversary, new hire announcement, upcoming conference
- Consider a brief voicemail to the practice (not personal cell) with a specific, relevant reason for the call

### Tier 3 — Long-Cycle Nurture (5+ contacts, consistently no response)
- Move to a low-frequency drip: 1 touch per quarter
- Add to a newsletter or market update distribution if one exists
- Flag for re-evaluation in 12–18 months — ownership situations change, partners retire, circumstances shift
- Do not permanently close out; re-prioritize when triggers emerge

### Closed Nurture Re-Engagement (Contacts who said "not now" or declined)
- These are the **highest-stakes relationships** — they have already formed an impression of AOSN
- Use **longer intervals** than standard re-engagement — months, not weeks
- Prefer **peer-to-peer** as the channel wherever possible
- **Never use campaign-style outreach** — must be highly personalized
- "Timing/not ready" contacts are different from hard nos — they've self-selected as understanding the value; they need patience, not pressure
- Re-engage when something meaningful changes: new close in their specialty, conference proximity, peer connection, life event trigger
- Contacts who went deep in the process (reached offer stage and declined) deserve a personalized, non-campaign touch when triggers emerge

## Creative Lead Generation Strategies

### Within Existing TAM
- **Mine referral networks from acquired practices** — ask current AOSN partners which independent peers they respect
- **Association membership directories** — AVDS, ACVD, ACVO, AVMA specialty sections for names not yet in pipeline
- **State veterinary board license lookups** — surface specialists not yet on radar
- **Conference attendance lists / speaking rosters** — credentialed specialists presenting are often owner-operators
- **LinkedIn Sales Navigator** — filter by specialty, years in practice, geography; identify owners vs. associates
- **Practice website audits** — identify single-DVM or 2-DVM practices without visible succession plans

### Trigger-Based Outreach
- **Doctor approaching retirement age** (estimated via vet school graduation year)
- **Practice recently lost a partner** — creates urgency around succession and workload
- **New facility construction or expansion** — may signal growth appetite or equity need
- **Recent awards or recognition** — provides a genuine, non-transactional reason to reach out
- **Job postings for associate positions** — may indicate growth or owner fatigue
- **Practice listed with a broker** — indicates active seller motivation; move quickly

### Network Expansion
- **Referral veterinarians (rDVMs)** as intelligence sources — they know which specialty owners are burning out
- **Veterinary practice brokers and consultants** — build relationships to get deal flow that doesn't fit their model
- **Veterinary lenders and CPAs** — often know when an owner is contemplating exit before anyone else does
- **State VMAs and local specialty societies** — attend meetings to build name recognition organically

### TAM Expansion Ideas (If Current TAM Is Exhausted)
- **Revisit TAM exclusions** — mobile surgeons, mixed-care hospitals with strong specialty components may be closer to the model than originally assumed
- **Go earlier on newly opened practices** — some may be further along than assumed, particularly older owners with strong early EBITDA
- **Associate outreach** — associate veterinarians sometimes want to buy in or take over; may surface conversations cold outreach to owners hasn't
- **Revisit early hard nos** — doctors who said no before AOSN had closed surgeons and ophthalmologists as proof points are working with different information now; a peer-to-peer call from a newly closed surgeon is a materially different conversation
- **Maximize EBITDA per close instead of NDA volume** — if volume is capped, ruthlessly deprioritize single-doctor practices and concentrate on Tier 1 multi-doctor targets

## Instructions for Claude When Using This Context

When given practice-level data (name, specialty, location, outreach history, last contact date, response status, EBITDA, doctor count, tier), use this document to:

1. **Recommend the single most appropriate next action** based on outreach stage, channel history, specialty context, tier, doctor count, and known triggers
2. **Draft outreach copy** that is personalized, low-pressure, and channel-appropriate — never generic, never corporate-sounding
3. **Assign a re-engagement tier** and recommend timing for the next touch, with special care for Closed Nurture contacts
4. **Suggest creative lead gen angles** if standard outreach has been exhausted
5. **Flag red flags** suggesting the target should be paused or deprioritized (single-doctor risk, valuation misalignment, over-touched, newly opened without viability assessment)
6. **Prioritize recommendations by EBITDA impact** — always favor multi-doctor Tier 1 targets over single-doctor practices when advising on where to spend time
7. **Factor in competitive pressure** — warm prospects moving slowly are at real risk of being closed by Mission Pet Health or Thrive before AOSN can advance
8. **Reference conference timing** — if a target's specialty has an upcoming conference, factor that into the recommended outreach approach

Always assume the goal is a long-term relationship, not a quick close. Tone should reflect that AOSN is a partner, not just a buyer. When the TAM is this finite, every touchpoint either builds or burns the relationship — there are no neutral interactions.
